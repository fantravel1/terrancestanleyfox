St. John's, Newfoundland

Where the Marathon of Hope Began - April 12, 1980

The Beginning

On the morning of April 12, 1980, at approximately 4:00 AM, a young man with curly brown hair and a prosthetic right leg stood on the rocky shores of St. John's, Newfoundland. Terry Fox, just 21 years old, dipped his artificial leg into the frigid waters of the Atlantic Ocean. With that simple act, he began what would become one of the most inspiring journeys in Canadian history‚Äîthe Marathon of Hope.

The moment was witnessed by only a handful of people, including his best friend and driver Doug Alward. There were no crowds, no cameras, no fanfare. Terry had deliberately chosen to start early and without publicity. He wanted the run to speak for itself.

"I'm not doing this for glory. I'm doing this to raise money for cancer research. If I can make it across Canada, then everyone else can help too." ‚Äî Terry Fox, April 12, 1980

Mile Zero

The starting point, now known as Mile Zero, was located at the edge of the Atlantic Ocean in St. John's harbour. Terry filled two large bottles with ocean water‚Äîone he would keep, and one he planned to pour into the Pacific Ocean when he reached Victoria, British Columbia. That second bottle would never complete its intended journey.

After the symbolic dip, Terry turned west and began running along Water Street. His distinctive hop-skip running style, caused by his prosthetic leg, would become one of the most recognizable images in Canadian history. He ran approximately 42 kilometers that first day, the equivalent of a full marathon‚Äîa distance he would maintain nearly every single day for the next 143 days.

Day 1 Statistics

  • Date: April 12, 1980 (Saturday)
  • Start Time: Approximately 4:00 AM
  • Starting Point: St. John's Harbour, Newfoundland
  • Distance Run: ~42 km (26 miles)
  • Weather: Cold, windy, with snow flurries
  • Witnesses: Doug Alward and a few locals
  • Media Coverage: Minimal - local newspaper only

Why St. John's?

Terry chose St. John's as his starting point because it is the easternmost city in North America. His goal was to run across the entire country, from the Atlantic Ocean to the Pacific Ocean. Starting at the eastern edge of Canada made the journey symbolically complete‚Äîhe would touch both oceans that bordered the nation.

The choice also meant facing some of the most challenging terrain first. Newfoundland's rugged landscape, unpredictable weather, and sparse population would test Terry immediately. There would be no gradual warm-up‚Äîthe Marathon of Hope began with hardship.

The First Days in Newfoundland

The initial days were brutal. Terry ran through cold rain, snow, and fierce winds. The roads were often hilly and poorly maintained. Few people knew about his mission, and donations were sparse. There were days when Terry and Doug wondered if anyone would ever notice.

Terry spent 28 days in Newfoundland, running approximately 900 kilometers before reaching the ferry to take him to Nova Scotia. During this time, he battled not just the elements but also mechanical problems with his prosthetic leg, blisters, and moments of profound loneliness.

"The first week was the hardest. Nobody knew who I was or what I was doing. I had to keep reminding myself why I started." ‚Äî Terry Fox, reflecting on Newfoundland

Growing Awareness

Slowly, word began to spread. Local newspapers picked up the story. Radio stations mentioned the young man running across their province. By the time Terry reached Port aux Basques to board the ferry to Nova Scotia, small crowds had begun to gather. The seeds of national attention were being planted.

Legacy of Mile Zero

Today, the spot where Terry dipped his leg into the Atlantic is marked by a monument. The Mile Zero monument in St. John's attracts visitors from around the world who come to pay tribute to Terry and to see where his incredible journey began.

Every September, Newfoundland hosts Terry Fox Runs, and many participants make the pilgrimage to Mile Zero before or after their run. The site has become sacred ground in Canadian history‚Äîa place where one person's determination changed a nation forever.
