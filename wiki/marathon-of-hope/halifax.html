Halifax, Nova Scotia

Gateway to the Maritimes - Late April 1980

Crossing to the Mainland

After spending 28 grueling days running across Newfoundland, Terry Fox took the ferry from Port aux Basques to North Sydney, Nova Scotia. From there, he continued his run toward Halifax, the largest city in Atlantic Canada.

The transition from Newfoundland to Nova Scotia marked a psychological milestone for Terry. He had conquered the first province‚Äîthe most isolated and challenging part of his journey. The mainland stretched before him, and with it, the hope of reaching more Canadians with his message.

Growing Recognition

By the time Terry reached Halifax in late April 1980, word of his journey had begun to spread through the Maritime provinces. Local newspapers had picked up the story, and radio stations were starting to mention the young amputee running across Canada.

Unlike the lonely roads of Newfoundland, Nova Scotia brought larger crowds and more media attention. People would stop their cars to donate money. Restaurants offered free meals. The Canadian Cancer Society's local chapters began organizing welcoming events.

"Nova Scotia was where I started to feel like maybe this could actually work. People were coming out to see me, to donate. It wasn't just me and Doug alone on the highway anymore." ‚Äî Terry Fox, on reaching Nova Scotia

The Maritime Spirit

Maritimers embraced Terry with characteristic warmth. Small-town mayors presented him with keys to their communities. School children lined roads with hand-painted signs. Fishermen, farmers, and factory workers dug into their pockets to support the cause.

The support was especially meaningful because Nova Scotia had its own high cancer rates, particularly in industrial Cape Breton. Many who came out to cheer Terry had lost loved ones to the disease. His mission resonated deeply with communities that understood his fight intimately.

Media Momentum

Halifax's newspapers‚Äîthe Chronicle Herald and the Daily News‚Äîgave Terry prominent coverage. The stories were syndicated to other Canadian Press member papers, spreading awareness across the country. This was the beginning of the national media attention that would eventually make Terry a household name.

Television crews from CBC Atlantic documented his run through the province, creating footage that would later be broadcast nationally. The visual image of Terry's distinctive running gait‚Äîhis hop-skip motion caused by his prosthetic leg‚Äîbecame instantly recognizable.

Challenges in Nova Scotia

Despite the improved reception, the physical challenges continued. The Cabot Trail in Cape Breton featured steep climbs and descents that tested Terry's endurance. His prosthetic leg continued to cause problems, requiring adjustments and repairs.

Doug Alward, driving the support van, became skilled at quick roadside repairs. The artificial leg's socket would rub raw spots on Terry's stump, and the running motion wore down components faster than anticipated.

Legacy in Nova Scotia

Today, Nova Scotia hosts dozens of Terry Fox Runs each September. Halifax's run is one of the largest in Atlantic Canada, with thousands participating annually. The province has contributed millions of dollars to cancer research through the Terry Fox Foundation.

Several schools and roads in Nova Scotia bear Terry's name, ensuring that new generations of Maritimers learn about the young man who ran through their province on his way to making history.
