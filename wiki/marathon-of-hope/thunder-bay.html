Thunder Bay, Ontario

Where the Marathon of Hope Ended - September 1, 1980

The Final Day

On the morning of September 1, 1980, Terry Fox woke up feeling different. After 143 days and 5,373 kilometers of running, his body was finally sending him a message he couldn't ignore. He had been coughing for days, and that morning, the pain in his chest was unbearable.

Terry tried to start his daily run anyway. He made it only a few kilometers before he had to stop. He turned to his friend Doug Alward in the support van and said the words that would mark the end of the Marathon of Hope: "Doug, I gotta stop."

The Diagnosis

Terry was rushed to a hospital in Thunder Bay, where X-rays confirmed his worst fear: the cancer had returned. The osteosarcoma that had claimed his leg three years earlier had now spread to his lungs. Doctors found tumors in both lungs.

The news spread across Canada like wildfire. The young man who had captured the nation's heart, who had run a marathon every single day through rain, snow, heat, and wind, was now fighting for his life. Canadians were devastated.

The Press Conference

Later that day, from his hospital bed, Terry held an emotional press conference that was broadcast across Canada. With tears in his eyes but resolve in his voice, he addressed the nation:

"I'm gonna do my very best. I'll fight. I promise I won't give up." ‚Äî Terry Fox, Press Conference, September 1, 1980

Even as he announced the end of his physical journey, Terry's thoughts were not on himself. He asked Canadians to continue supporting cancer research, to continue donating, to continue the mission he had started. The Marathon of Hope, he insisted, was not over‚Äîit was just beginning a new chapter.

The Nation Responds

The response was immediate and overwhelming. Donations that had been trickling in suddenly became a flood. The Canadian Cancer Society phone lines were jammed. Canadians who had been inspired by Terry's run now wanted to help him reach his goal of raising one dollar from every Canadian.

Isadore Sharp, founder of Four Seasons Hotels and himself a cancer survivor's father, organized a national telethon. Within days, donations poured in from across the country. By the time Terry returned to Port Coquitlam, his goal of $24 million had been surpassed.

The Terry Fox Memorial

The exact spot where Terry stopped running is now marked by the Terry Fox Memorial, one of Canada's most visited monuments. A nine-foot bronze statue of Terry, created by sculptor Manfred Pirwitz, stands overlooking Lake Superior, forever frozen in his distinctive running pose.

The memorial site, located on the Terry Fox Scenic Lookout along Highway 17 (now renamed the Terry Fox Courage Highway), draws hundreds of thousands of visitors each year. Many Canadians consider it a pilgrimage to visit the spot where Terry's physical journey ended but his legacy truly began.

The Inscription

The memorial plaque reads: "Terry Fox - Marathon of Hope. 'I just wish people would realize that anything's possible if you try. Dreams are made possible if you try.'"

Aftermath

Terry was flown back to British Columbia, where he began aggressive cancer treatment. Despite the best medical care available, the cancer was too advanced. Terry passed away on June 28, 1981, just one month before his 23rd birthday.

But his mission lived on. The first Terry Fox Run was held on September 13, 1981, with over 300,000 participants. Today, Terry Fox Runs are held annually in more than 60 countries, and the total raised in Terry's name has exceeded $850 million.
