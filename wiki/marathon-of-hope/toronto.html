Toronto, Ontario

The Triumphant Arrival - July 11, 1980

The Turning Point

July 11, 1980 marked the most significant day of the Marathon of Hope. After 86 days and over 3,500 kilometers of running, Terry Fox arrived in Toronto to a reception that exceeded anything he had imagined. At Nathan Phillips Square, an estimated 10,000 people had gathered to welcome him.

The crowd roared as Terry ran into the square, his curly hair bouncing with each distinctive hop-skip stride. This was the moment when the Marathon of Hope transformed from a personal mission into a national movement.

"I couldn't believe it. All those people, all that support. It was overwhelming. This is why I'm doing this." ‚Äî Terry Fox, July 11, 1980

Nathan Phillips Square

The celebration at Nathan Phillips Square was orchestrated by the Canadian Cancer Society and Toronto city officials. Terry was welcomed by Mayor John Sewell, who presented him with a key to the city. Hockey legend Darryl Sittler of the Toronto Maple Leafs joined Terry on stage, presenting him with his 1980 NHL All-Star jersey.

The crowd included cancer survivors, families who had lost loved ones to the disease, and thousands of Torontonians who simply wanted to show their support. Terry addressed the crowd, his voice breaking with emotion as he thanked them for believing in his mission.

Toronto Celebration Details

  • Date: Friday, July 11, 1980
  • Location: Nathan Phillips Square, Toronto City Hall
  • Attendance: Approximately 10,000 people
  • Notable Attendees: Mayor John Sewell, Darryl Sittler
  • Gifts Received: Key to the City, Sittler's All-Star jersey
  • Media Coverage: National television broadcast

Meeting Darryl Sittler

One of the most memorable moments of Terry's time in Toronto was meeting Darryl Sittler, captain of the Toronto Maple Leafs. Sittler, himself moved by Terry's courage, gave Terry his 1980 NHL All-Star Game jersey right off his back.

"I've met a lot of brave people in my life," Sittler said to the crowd, "but I've never met anyone as brave as Terry Fox." The gesture symbolized how Terry had captured the hearts of even Canada's greatest sports heroes.

"Terry Fox is the real all-star. What he's doing is more important than any hockey game." ‚Äî Darryl Sittler, Toronto Maple Leafs Captain

National Attention

The Toronto visit was broadcast live on CBC Television, bringing Terry's story into living rooms across Canada. For many Canadians outside Ontario, this was their first glimpse of the young man they had been reading about in newspapers. The visual impact was profound‚Äîseeing Terry run on one leg moved millions to tears and inspired unprecedented donations.

Following Toronto, donations to the Marathon of Hope exploded. The Canadian Cancer Society reported that their phone lines were overwhelmed with callers wanting to contribute. Corporate sponsors began reaching out. The marathon had become a national phenomenon.

Isadore Sharp's Promise

It was around this time that Isadore Sharp, founder of Four Seasons Hotels, made a commitment that would shape Terry's legacy. Sharp, who had lost his own son to cancer, pledged to organize an annual run in Terry's name if Terry couldn't finish the marathon. This promise would become the Terry Fox Run.

Betty Fox in Toronto

Betty Fox, Terry's mother, flew to Toronto to be with her son during the celebration. It was an emotional reunion‚ÄîBetty had followed Terry's progress anxiously from Port Coquitlam, and seeing the national outpouring of support brought her to tears.

"I always knew Terry was special," Betty said, "but I never imagined something like this. Canada has embraced my son, and I'll never forget it."

Running Through Ontario

Terry spent several weeks running through Ontario, Canada's most populous province. The support continued to grow as he passed through cities and towns along Highway 17. Every stop brought new crowds, new donations, and new stories of how Terry's courage had touched lives.

The Ontario stretch was physically demanding‚Äîlong, hot summer days on busy highways. But the constant encouragement from supporters gave Terry renewed energy. He was no longer running alone; an entire nation was running with him.

Legacy in Toronto

Today, Toronto hosts one of the largest Terry Fox Runs in the world. Nathan Phillips Square remains a gathering point for the annual event, with thousands retracing the path where Terry once stood. The city has named streets, schools, and parks in his honor.

A permanent plaque at City Hall commemorates Terry's 1980 visit, reminding visitors of the day when a young man from British Columbia united an entire city‚Äîand inspired a nation.
