Port Coquitlam, BC

Terry Fox's Hometown

Overview

Port Coquitlam, affectionately known as "PoCo," is a city in the Lower Mainland of British Columbia, Canada. With a population of approximately 60,000, it sits at the confluence of the Coquitlam and Fraser Rivers, about 27 kilometers east of Vancouver.

For the Fox family, Port Coquitlam was home. It was here that Terry Fox grew up, attended school, played sports, and developed the character that would define his remarkable life. The city has never forgotten its most famous son.

Quick Facts

  • Location: Lower Mainland, British Columbia
  • Population: ~60,000
  • Distance from Vancouver: 27 km east
  • Fox Family Residence: Morrill Street
  • Terry's Schools: Banting, Mary Hill, Port Coquitlam Secondary
  • Famous For: Terry Fox's hometown

The Fox Family Home

The Fox family moved to Port Coquitlam from Winnipeg, Manitoba in the mid-1960s, when Terry was about eight years old. They settled into a modest house on Morrill Street in a working-class neighborhood.

The house was typical of the area‚Äîa small family home with a yard where Terry and his siblings could play. Rolland Fox worked various jobs to support the family of six, while Betty Fox raised their four children: Fred, Terry, Darrell, and Judith.

"Port Coquitlam was a great place to grow up. We had everything we needed‚Äîgood schools, good neighbors, and lots of space to run around and play sports." ‚Äî Terry Fox

Terry's Schools

Terry's education in Port Coquitlam shaped his character and introduced him to the sports he loved:

Banting Middle School

Terry attended Banting Middle School, where he first developed his passion for sports. Though not naturally gifted athletically, Terry made up for it with determination and hard work. He played on school sports teams and began to show the competitive spirit that would later define him.

Mary Hill Junior Secondary

At Mary Hill Junior Secondary, Terry continued to pursue athletics, particularly basketball. His physical education teacher, Bob McGill, recognized Terry's determination and work ethic, even when his skills lagged behind more naturally talented players.

Port Coquitlam Secondary

Terry graduated from Port Coquitlam Secondary School in 1976. During his high school years, he became a standout athlete through sheer determination. He co-won the Athlete of the Year award in his graduating year‚Äîa testament to his incredible work ethic.

It was also at Port Coquitlam Secondary that Terry met Doug Alward, who would become his best friend and later drive the support van during the Marathon of Hope.

Training Grounds

After Terry's amputation in 1977, Port Coquitlam became his training ground for the Marathon of Hope. He ran countless miles through the streets and trails of his hometown, building his endurance on the same roads where he had played as a child.

Local residents grew accustomed to seeing Terry running with his prosthetic leg, his distinctive hop-skip gait becoming a familiar sight in the community. The support of his neighbors gave Terry confidence that his mission might resonate with all of Canada.

Terry Fox Tributes in Port Coquitlam

Port Coquitlam has honored Terry extensively since his death in 1981:

Local Tributes

  • Terry Fox Library: Named in his honor
  • Terry Fox Elementary School: Opened in his memory
  • Terry Fox Hometown Run: Annual event in his hometown
  • Terry Fox Statue: Bronze statue in the city center
  • Street Names: Terry Fox Way and related streets
  • Memorial Plaques: At his former schools

The Terry Fox Hometown Run

Each September, Port Coquitlam hosts the Terry Fox Hometown Run, one of the most significant Terry Fox Runs in Canada. Participants run through the same streets where Terry grew up, trained, and dreamed of making a difference.

The event attracts thousands of participants, including many who knew Terry personally. The Fox family has historically attended the Hometown Run, making it a particularly meaningful event in the annual calendar of Terry Fox events.

Betty Fox's Final Years

Betty Fox continued to live in Port Coquitlam until her death in June 2024. For over 40 years after Terry's death, she remained a fixture in the community, attending local events and keeping Terry's memory alive.

The community embraced Betty as their own, protecting her privacy while celebrating her tireless work for the Terry Fox Foundation. Her presence in Port Coquitlam served as a living connection to Terry's legacy.
