Terry Fox Memorial

Thunder Bay, Ontario - Where the Marathon Lives On

Overview

The Terry Fox Memorial is a monument located just outside Thunder Bay, Ontario, near the spot where Terry Fox was forced to end his Marathon of Hope on September 1, 1980. The memorial features a 2.7-meter (9-foot) bronze sculpture of Terry, captured mid-stride, overlooking Lake Superior.

Since its unveiling in 1982, the memorial has become one of Canada's most visited monuments, drawing hundreds of thousands of visitors each year who come to pay their respects to the young man who inspired a nation.

  • Unveiled: June 26, 1982
  • Sculptor: Manfred Pirwitz
  • Height: 2.7 meters (9 feet)

The Sculpture

The bronze sculpture was created by Canadian artist Manfred Pirwitz. It depicts Terry in his iconic running pose‚Äîleaning forward, curly hair flowing, his prosthetic leg extended in the distinctive hop-skip motion that carried him across thousands of kilometers.

Pirwitz studied films and photographs of Terry extensively to capture his movement accurately. The result is a dynamic sculpture that seems to capture Terry mid-stride, frozen in the act that made him famous but still conveying motion and determination.

"I wanted to capture Terry's determination, his forward motion. He was always moving forward, never looking back. That's what I tried to show." ‚Äî Manfred Pirwitz, sculptor

The Location

The memorial sits on a rocky outcrop overlooking Lake Superior, near the spot on Highway 17 where Terry was forced to stop running. The location was chosen both for its proximity to the Marathon's end point and for its stunning natural beauty.

The view from the memorial is spectacular. Lake Superior stretches to the horizon, and on clear days, visitors can see for miles in every direction. The setting reflects the scale of Terry's achievement‚Äîvast, beautiful, and seemingly endless.

Visitor Experience

The Terry Fox Memorial and Lookout is a designated rest area along the Trans-Canada Highway. Visitors can park and walk to the sculpture, which is surrounded by interpretive plaques telling Terry's story.

  • Interpretive Plaques: The story of the Marathon of Hope
  • Quote Wall: Terry's famous words
  • Viewing Platform: Overlooking Lake Superior
  • Information Centre: Seasonal exhibits about Terry

A Place of Pilgrimage

For many Canadians, visiting the Terry Fox Memorial has become a pilgrimage. Families stop during cross-country road trips to show their children where Terry's run ended. Cancer survivors come to draw inspiration. Athletes visit before major events to connect with Terry's spirit.

Visitors often leave tokens at the base of the statue‚Äîrunning shoes, letters, flowers, Canadian flags. These offerings are periodically collected and preserved by the site's caretakers, testament to the emotional impact Terry continues to have.

The Unveiling

The memorial was unveiled on June 26, 1982, almost exactly one year after Terry's death. The ceremony was attended by the Fox family, government officials, and thousands of supporters who had traveled to Thunder Bay for the occasion.

Betty Fox unveiled the sculpture, drawing back a cloth to reveal her son captured in bronze for eternity. It was an emotional moment for the family and for Canada.

Other Terry Fox Memorials

While the Thunder Bay memorial is the most famous, Terry is commemorated at numerous locations across Canada:

Mile Zero, St. John's: A plaque marks the spot where Terry began the Marathon of Hope.
Parliament Hill, Ottawa: A statue of Terry stands near the entrance.
BC Place, Vancouver: A statue greets visitors at the stadium.
Simon Fraser University: Terry's alma mater has a memorial on campus.
Beacon Hill Park, Victoria: Terry Fox Plaza features another sculpture.

Visiting Information

The Terry Fox Memorial and Lookout is accessible year-round, though the visitor centre operates seasonally. The site is located approximately 80 kilometers east of Thunder Bay on Highway 17, the Trans-Canada Highway.

There is no admission fee. The memorial is a place for quiet reflection, and visitors are encouraged to take time to read Terry's words and contemplate his achievement.
