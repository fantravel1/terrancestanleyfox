Terry Fox's Childhood

The Early Years That Shaped a Hero (1958-1976)

Birth and Early Infancy

Terrance Stanley Fox was born on July 28, 1958, at Winnipeg's Grace Hospital in Manitoba, Canada.[1] He was the second child of Rolland Fox, a switchman for the Canadian National Railway, and Betty Lou Fox (n√©e Chicken). Terry's older brother Fred had been born two years earlier in 1956.

The name "Terrance" was chosen by his parents, though he would be known as "Terry" throughout his life. His middle name "Stanley" was a family name passed down through generations. From birth, Terry was described by his mother as an active, curious baby who rarely sat still.[2]

The Move West

When Terry was approximately eight years old, the Fox family made the significant decision to move west to British Columbia.[3] Rolland Fox had secured a transfer with the Canadian National Railway, and the family settled first in Surrey, BC, before eventually moving to Port Coquitlam, a suburban city east of Vancouver.

The move to British Columbia would prove to be one of the most significant events in Terry's life. Port Coquitlam, with its strong community sports programs and outdoor recreation opportunities, provided the perfect environment for Terry to develop his athletic abilities and the determination that would later define him.

"Terry was never the biggest or the fastest, but he was always the most determined. Even as a child, if he set his mind to something, nothing could stop him." ‚Äî Betty Fox, Terry's mother[4]

Childhood Personality

From an early age, Terry exhibited the personality traits that would later make him a national hero. Family members and childhood friends consistently described him as incredibly determined, sometimes to the point of stubbornness. He refused to accept defeat and would practice tirelessly to improve at anything he attempted.

Terry was known for his competitive spirit, but also for his sense of fair play and genuine kindness toward others. His brother Darrell later recalled that Terry was protective of his younger siblings and would stand up for other children who were being bullied.[5]

Early Athletic Interests

Sports became Terry's primary passion from a young age. Though he was not naturally gifted with exceptional height or speed, he compensated through sheer determination and endless practice. He participated in soccer, rugby, and baseball, but it was basketball that would become his greatest love.

At Mary Hill Junior Secondary School, Terry tried out for the basketball team despite being smaller than most of his peers. Though he didn't initially make the team, he practiced relentlessly and eventually earned a spot through pure dedication.[6]

Elementary and Junior High School

Terry attended elementary school in Port Coquitlam before moving on to Mary Hill Junior Secondary School. His teachers remembered him as a hardworking student who, while not exceptionally academic, approached his schoolwork with the same determination he showed in sports.

High School Years

Terry attended Port Coquitlam Senior Secondary School from 1973 to 1976. These years were transformative, as Terry developed both as an athlete and as a person. He played on the basketball team alongside his close friend Doug Alward, who would later drive the support van during the Marathon of Hope.[7]

Physical education teacher Bob McGill became an important mentor to Terry during these years. McGill recognized Terry's exceptional determination and work ethic, later stating that while Terry wasn't the most talented athlete he had taught, he was certainly the most determined.[8]

"I've coached hundreds of athletes over my career. Terry wasn't the most naturally gifted, but I've never seen anyone work harder. He simply refused to quit, no matter how difficult things got." ‚Äî Bob McGill, Terry's PE teacher[8]

Formative Influences

Several key influences shaped Terry during his childhood years. His parents instilled in him a strong work ethic and the belief that with enough effort, anything was possible. The community of Port Coquitlam provided a supportive environment where sports and outdoor activities were valued.

Terry's determination was also shaped by the challenges he faced. Being smaller than his peers in sports taught him that natural ability wasn't everything‚Äîdedication and practice could overcome physical limitations. This lesson would prove prophetic when he later undertook the Marathon of Hope.

Values and Character Development

The Fox family emphasized honesty, hard work, and helping others. Terry absorbed these values and they became central to his character. Friends remembered him as someone who kept his word and always tried to help those in need.

These childhood years laid the foundation for the man Terry would become‚Äîsomeone who, when faced with cancer and amputation, would choose to run across Canada not for personal glory, but to help others facing the same disease.
