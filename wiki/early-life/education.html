Terry Fox's Education

From Port Coquitlam Schools to Simon Fraser University

Overview

Terry Fox's educational journey took him through the public schools of Port Coquitlam, British Columbia and on to Simon Fraser University, where he was studying Kinesiology when he was diagnosed with cancer. Throughout his academic career, Terry was known less for exceptional grades than for his exceptional determination and work ethic.

Educational Timeline

  • Elementary: Schools in Winnipeg and Port Coquitlam
  • Middle School: Banting Middle School, Port Coquitlam
  • Junior Secondary: Mary Hill Junior Secondary School
  • High School: Port Coquitlam Secondary School (Class of 1976)
  • University: Simon Fraser University, Kinesiology (1976-1977)

Elementary and Middle School

Terry began his education in Winnipeg, Manitoba, where he was born. When the Fox family moved to British Columbia in the mid-1960s, Terry transferred into the Port Coquitlam school district.

At Banting Middle School, Terry first discovered his love of sports. He wasn't the most naturally gifted athlete‚Äîin fact, he was often smaller and less coordinated than his classmates‚Äîbut he made up for it with relentless practice and determination.

"Terry wasn't the biggest or the fastest, but he was always the hardest worker. He'd stay after practice, run extra laps, shoot extra baskets. That's just who he was." ‚Äî Childhood friend

Mary Hill Junior Secondary

At Mary Hill Junior Secondary School, Terry continued to develop as both a student and athlete. It was here that he met Bob McGill, a physical education teacher who would become an important mentor.

McGill recognized Terry's exceptional determination and encouraged him to pursue basketball despite his smaller stature. This encouragement planted seeds that would grow throughout Terry's life‚Äîthe belief that determination could overcome physical limitations.

Port Coquitlam Secondary School

Terry attended Port Coquitlam Secondary School (often called "Poco Secondary") from 1973 to 1976. These were formative years where his character truly emerged.

Academic Performance

Terry was a solid but not exceptional student academically. He maintained respectable grades while dedicating significant time to athletics. His teachers noted his determination and focus‚Äîqualities that were more evident on the basketball court than in the classroom.

Athletic Achievements

High school was where Terry's athletic determination truly shone. He tried out for the basketball team in Grade 8 and didn't make it. Rather than give up, he practiced relentlessly over the summer. By Grade 10, he had made the team. By his senior year, he was co-captain.

High School Achievements

  • Basketball: Co-captain of the school team
  • Cross-Country: Participated in distance running
  • Athlete of the Year: Co-winner in graduating year (1976)
  • Work Ethic: Known for staying late at practices

Athlete of the Year

In 1976, Terry co-won the Athlete of the Year award at Port Coquitlam Secondary. This recognition wasn't for being the most talented athlete‚Äîit was for embodying the spirit of athletics through dedication, teamwork, and perseverance.

The award was shared with his friend Doug Alward, who would later become Terry's companion on the Marathon of Hope. Their friendship, forged on the playing fields of Poco Secondary, would prove unbreakable.

Simon Fraser University

After graduating from high school in 1976, Terry enrolled at Simon Fraser University (SFU) in Burnaby, British Columbia. He chose to study Kinesiology‚Äîthe science of human movement‚Äîreflecting his passion for athletics and physical fitness.

Choosing Kinesiology

Terry's choice of Kinesiology was deliberate. He wanted to understand how the human body worked, how athletes could optimize their performance, and potentially pursue a career in physical education or coaching. The irony that this knowledge would later help him understand his own body's response to cancer and amputation was not lost on those who knew him.

University Basketball

At SFU, Terry continued playing basketball at a competitive level. The university's team was more talented than any he had faced in high school, but Terry's determination remained unchanged. He worked just as hard to earn playing time at the university level as he had in Grade 8.

Cancer Diagnosis

Terry's university career was cut short by his cancer diagnosis in November 1976, during his first year at SFU. The osteosarcoma in his right knee led to his leg amputation in March 1977.

Despite his illness and amputation, Terry returned to SFU after his treatment. He was determined to complete his education, even as he began training for what would become the Marathon of Hope.

"My education didn't stop when I lost my leg. I learned more about myself during that time than I ever did in a classroom. I learned what I was capable of." ‚Äî Terry Fox

The Marathon and Education

Terry took a leave from SFU to attempt the Marathon of Hope in 1980. His plan was to return to his studies after completing the run across Canada. Sadly, the return of his cancer prevented him from ever completing his degree.

However, Terry's educational legacy lives on through the countless schools named in his honor across Canada and around the world. These institutions teach students not just academics, but the values Terry embodied: perseverance, courage, and selflessness.
