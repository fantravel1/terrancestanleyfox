Canada Loses a Hero

June 28, 1981

The End of a Remarkable Life

On Sunday, June 28, 1981, at 4:35 AM, Terrance Stanley Fox passed away at Royal Columbian Hospital in New Westminster, British Columbia. He was 22 years old‚Äîexactly one month before what would have been his 23rd birthday.

Terry died surrounded by his family: his parents Betty and Rolland, and his siblings Fred, Darrell, and Judith. He had fought cancer with the same determination he had shown during the Marathon of Hope, but in the end, the disease proved too aggressive.

A Nation Mourns

News of Terry's death spread quickly across Canada. By morning, the country was in mourning. Flags on Parliament Hill and at government buildings across the nation were lowered to half-staff‚Äîan honor normally reserved for heads of state.

Prime Minister Pierre Trudeau issued a statement: "It occurs very rarely in the life of a nation that the weights and measures of a people are changed by the life and death of one citizen. Terry Fox changed us. He gave us pride in ourselves."

The Final Months

After being forced to stop the Marathon near Thunder Bay on September 1, 1980, Terry had returned home to British Columbia. He underwent aggressive cancer treatment, but the disease had spread to his lungs and was not responding.

Despite his illness, Terry continued to advocate for cancer research from his hospital bed. He gave interviews, recorded messages, and watched with joy as Canadians surpassed his fundraising goal of $24 million‚Äî$1 for every Canadian.

In his final weeks, Terry was at peace. He knew that while he couldn't finish the Marathon himself, his mission would continue.

The Funeral

Terry's funeral was held on July 2, 1981, at the Scottish Cultural Centre in Port Coquitlam. Thousands of mourners gathered, including dignitaries, athletes, and ordinary Canadians whose lives Terry had touched.

The service was broadcast nationally on CBC Television. Canadians from coast to coast watched as the nation said goodbye to the young man who had inspired them.

Immediate Response

The outpouring of grief was accompanied by renewed commitment to Terry's cause. Within days of his death, Isadore Sharp began organizing the first Terry Fox Run, scheduled for September 1981.

Donations to the Marathon of Hope continued to pour in. By the end of 1981, the fund had reached over $23 million. Terry's goal‚Äî$1 from every Canadian‚Äîhad been achieved and surpassed.

Legacy Begins

Even in death, Terry's influence grew. In September 1981, just three months after his passing, over 300,000 Canadians participated in the first Terry Fox Run, raising $3.5 million.

The run would become an annual tradition, eventually spreading to over 60 countries. To date, more than $850 million has been raised in Terry's name for cancer research.

Schools, roads, mountains, and buildings across Canada have been named in Terry's honor. He was named the greatest Canadian in a 2004 CBC poll. His story continues to inspire new generations.
