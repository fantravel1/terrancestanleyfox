A Canadian Hero is Born

The Fox Family

Terry was the second of four children born to Rolland and Betty Fox. His older brother Fred had been born a few years earlier, and Terry would later be joined by younger brother Darrell and sister Judith.

The Fox family was of English and Scottish descent, part of the wave of immigrants who had settled the Canadian prairies in earlier generations. They were not wealthy, but they were hardworking and close-knit.

Winnipeg in 1958

The Winnipeg of 1958 was a city of approximately 450,000 people, serving as the commercial hub of the Canadian prairies. It was a place where community mattered, where neighbors helped neighbors, and where children played freely in streets and parks.

Canada in 1958 was experiencing post-war prosperity. The economy was growing, suburban developments were expanding, and families were optimistic about the future. It was into this hopeful environment that Terry was born.

Early Years in Winnipeg

Terry spent the first eight years of his life in Winnipeg before the family moved to British Columbia. These early years on the prairies shaped his character in ways that would become apparent later in life.

The harsh Manitoba winters taught resilience. The strong community bonds taught the value of helping others. The modest family circumstances taught that hard work, not privilege, was the path to achievement.

The Move West

In the mid-1960s, when Terry was about eight years old, the Fox family made the decision to move west to British Columbia, seeking better opportunities. They settled in Port Coquitlam, a suburb of Vancouver, where Terry would spend the rest of his childhood.

This move marked a new chapter in Terry's life, but the values instilled during his Winnipeg years‚Äîdetermination, community, and perseverance‚Äîremained with him always.

A Life Yet to Unfold

In July 1958, no one could have predicted what Terrance Stanley Fox would accomplish in his short life. The baby born that summer day in Winnipeg would grow up to inspire millions, raise awareness about cancer research, and become a symbol of courage and determination for generations of Canadians.

Terry would live only 22 years, but his legacy continues to grow more than four decades after his death. Every Terry Fox Run, every dollar raised for cancer research, traces back to this moment‚Äîthe birth of a boy who would change Canada forever.
