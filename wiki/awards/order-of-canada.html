Canada's Highest Civilian Honor

The Award

On September 18, 1980‚Äîjust seventeen days after being forced to end his Marathon of Hope due to the return of cancer‚ÄîTerry Fox was made a Companion of the Order of Canada, the highest level of Canada's foremost civilian honor.

About the Order of Canada

The Order of Canada was established in 1967 by Queen Elizabeth II to recognize outstanding achievement, dedication to the community, and service to Canada. It is the centerpiece of Canada's honors system.

The Order has three levels:

Levels of the Order of Canada

  • Companion (C.C.): Highest level, for outstanding achievement and merit of the highest degree, especially in service to Canada or humanity. Limited to 165 living members.
  • Officer (O.C.): For achievement and merit of a high degree, especially in service to Canada or humanity at national or international level.
  • Member (C.M.): For distinguished service in or to a particular locality, group, or field of activity.

The Companion level is reserved for the most exceptional Canadians. At any given time, only 165 living Canadians can hold this designation (excluding the Governor General). Being named a Companion places Terry among the most honored Canadians in history.

The Ceremony

Due to Terry's declining health, the Order of Canada investiture was held as a special ceremony rather than at the traditional Government House event. Governor General Edward Schreyer traveled to meet Terry and present the award.

Terry received the insignia‚Äîa stylized snowflake bearing the Crown and a maple leaf with the Latin motto "Desiderantes Meliorem Patriam" (They desire a better country)‚Äîwhile surrounded by his family. The moment was bittersweet, celebrating Terry's achievement while his health continued to deteriorate.

"This award belongs to every Canadian who supported the Marathon of Hope. I just hope I can keep fighting and that we can find a cure for cancer." ‚Äî Terry Fox, upon receiving the Order of Canada

Youngest Companion

At 22 years and 52 days old, Terry Fox became the youngest person to be named a Companion of the Order of Canada at that time. This record reflected the extraordinary impact he had made in such a short period of time.

The decision to award Terry the Order's highest level was unanimous. His journey had touched virtually every Canadian, and his selfless determination to fight cancer‚Äîeven as he battled the disease himself‚Äîexemplified the values the Order was created to honor.

The Citation

The official citation for Terry's Companion of the Order of Canada reads:

"Terrance Stanley Fox is being invested as a Companion of the Order of Canada in recognition of his remarkable courage and determination in running across Canada to raise funds for cancer research. Despite losing a leg to cancer, he set out to run from coast to coast, inspiring millions of Canadians with his spirit and determination. His Marathon of Hope has raised unprecedented funds for cancer research and united Canadians in a common cause." ‚Äî Official Citation, Order of Canada

Posthumous Impact

Terry's Order of Canada recognition helped solidify his place in Canadian history. After his death in June 1981, the award served as a permanent reminder of how profoundly he had affected the nation.

Today, Terry's Companion designation is cited in virtually every official biography and tribute. The award validated what Canadians already knew‚Äîthat Terry Fox was one of the most remarkable citizens the country had ever produced.
