The Terry Fox Story

The Complete Biography of Canada's Greatest Hero

The inspiring journey of Terrance Stanley Fox (1958-1981)

Early Life (1958-1976)

Terrance Stanley "Terry" Fox was born on July 28, 1958, in Winnipeg, Manitoba, Canada. He was the second of four children born to Rolland and Betty Fox. When Terry was young, his family moved to Surrey, British Columbia, and later to Port Coquitlam, where he would grow up and attend school.

From an early age, Terry showed an incredible determination and love for sports. He was particularly passionate about basketball and participated in many team sports at Mary Hill Junior Secondary School and later at Port Coquitlam Senior Secondary School. Despite being shorter than many of his teammates, his work ethic and determination made him a valuable player.

His physical education teacher, Bob McGill, recognized Terry's exceptional drive and later said Terry was not the most talented athlete, but he was certainly the most determined. This quality would define Terry's entire life.

"Dreams are made possible if you try." ‚Äî Terry Fox

After graduating from high school, Terry enrolled at Simon Fraser University to study kinesiology. He continued playing basketball and was a member of the university's junior varsity team. His life seemed set on a normal path‚Äîuntil everything changed in November 1976.

The Diagnosis (1976-1977)

In November 1976, Terry began experiencing pain in his right knee. At first, he thought it was just a sports injury from basketball. But when the pain persisted and worsened, he finally went to see a doctor. The diagnosis was devastating: osteosarcoma, a form of bone cancer.

On March 9, 1977, at just 18 years old, Terry's right leg was amputated 15 centimeters (6 inches) above the knee. The night before his amputation, he read an article about Dick Traum, an amputee who had completed the New York City Marathon. This planted a seed that would grow into something extraordinary.

The Hospital Experience That Changed Everything

During his time in the hospital receiving chemotherapy, Terry saw the suffering of other cancer patients, including many children. He later wrote in a letter to the Canadian Cancer Society:

"I was only 18 years old. I thought I was dying. I wondered why this happened to me and not someone else. Somewhere the hurting must stop... and I was determined to take myself out of this environment to prove that cancer could be beaten."

Just three weeks after his surgery, Terry was fitted with an artificial leg and began walking. His recovery was remarkable‚Äîhe refused to let the amputation slow him down. Within months, he was not just walking but running.

A Dream Takes Shape (1977-1980)

During his 16 months of chemotherapy treatment, Terry began secretly training to run. He participated in his first marathon‚Äîthe Prince George Labatt Marathon in August 1979‚Äîfinishing last but completing the full 42 kilometers. This proved to himself that his dream was possible.

Terry conceived the Marathon of Hope: he would run across Canada, from coast to coast, to raise money and awareness for cancer research. His goal was ambitious yet simple‚Äîhe wanted to raise one dollar from every Canadian. With a population of approximately 24 million, that meant raising $24 million.

Terry trained intensively for 18 months, running over 5,300 kilometers to prepare. He wrote letters to corporations asking for sponsorship and support. He was initially rejected by many, but eventually secured backing from the Canadian Cancer Society and Ford of Canada, which donated a van.

His friend Doug Alward agreed to drive the support van that would accompany Terry across the country. Everything was set. On April 12, 1980, Terry would begin his incredible journey.

The Marathon of Hope (April - September 1980)

On April 12, 1980, Terry dipped his artificial leg in the Atlantic Ocean at St. John's, Newfoundland, and began running west. His goal: to dip it in the Pacific Ocean in Victoria, British Columbia, after crossing the entire country.

The early days were tough. Terry ran through cold, rain, and snow with little fanfare. Few people knew about his mission. He faced loneliness, physical pain, and doubt. But he kept running‚Äîapproximately 42 kilometers (a full marathon) every single day.

Growing Momentum

As Terry ran through the Maritime provinces and into Quebec and Ontario, word of his mission spread. Media coverage increased. Crowds began gathering to cheer him on. Donations poured in.

On July 1, 1980 (Canada Day), Terry was greeted by thousands of supporters. He met Canadian hockey legend Bobby Orr and other celebrities who championed his cause. By the time he reached Toronto, Terry was a national hero.

"Even if I don't finish, we need others to continue. It's got to keep going without me." ‚Äî Terry Fox

The Daily Struggle

Each day followed a grueling routine. Terry woke before dawn and ran until mid-morning, stopped for a break, then ran again until evening. His prosthetic leg caused painful blisters and sores. He ran through all weather‚Äîrain, wind, extreme heat‚Äînever taking a day off.

Despite the physical toll, Terry refused to stop. He was driven by the faces he'd seen in the cancer ward, the children fighting for their lives, the families devastated by the disease. Every step was for them.

The Tragic End (September 1980)

On the morning of September 1, 1980, outside Thunder Bay, Ontario, Terry woke up feeling unwell. He was coughing and had chest pains. He tried to continue running but had to stop after just a few kilometers.

"I gotta stop," he told Doug Alward. An ambulance took him to a hospital, where doctors delivered the devastating news: the cancer had spread to his lungs. After 143 days and 5,373 kilometers, the Marathon of Hope was over.

In a tearful press conference, Terry spoke to the nation: "I'm gonna do my very best. I'll fight, I promise I won't give up." Even in that moment, his thoughts were not on himself but on the mission. He asked Canadians to continue supporting cancer research.

The response was overwhelming. Donations flooded in. A telethon raised millions. By the time Terry returned home to British Columbia, his original goal of $24 million had been surpassed.

His Final Months & Legacy

Terry returned home to Port Coquitlam and continued fighting. He received the Companion of the Order of Canada, the nation's highest civilian honor. He was named Canadian of the Year and received the Lou Marsh Trophy as Canada's top athlete.

But despite aggressive treatment, the cancer was too advanced. On June 28, 1981‚Äîone month before his 23rd birthday‚ÄîTerry Fox passed away surrounded by his family.

The Terry Fox Run

On September 13, 1981‚Äîless than three months after Terry's death‚Äîthe first Terry Fox Run was held. Over 300,000 Canadians participated, raising $3.5 million. It became an annual tradition that continues today.

The Terry Fox Run is now held in more than 60 countries every year. To date, over $850 million has been raised for cancer research in Terry's name‚Äîmore than 35 times his original goal.

Memorials & Tributes

Terry Fox's legacy lives on in countless ways. Schools, roads, and buildings across Canada bear his name. The Terry Fox Memorial in Thunder Bay marks where his run ended. His story is taught in schools, and his determination continues to inspire new generations.

"I just wish people would realize that anything's possible if you try; dreams are made possible if you try." ‚Äî Terry Fox

Terry Fox ran into the hearts of Canadians and people around the world. His Marathon of Hope may have ended at Thunder Bay, but his legacy runs on forever.
